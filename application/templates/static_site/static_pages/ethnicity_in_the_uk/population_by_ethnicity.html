Population of England and Wales by ethnicity

To put the statistics about ethnicity in context, it’s useful to understand the ethnic makeup of the general population. This helps identify significant differences between ethnic groups in accessing public services.

For example, the finding that 78% of learners in further education in 2015 were from a White background doesn’t tell the full story on its own. But the fact that the White share of the general population is 86%, allows that finding to be read in a wider context.

How the government collects ethnicity data

A Census is held in the UK every 10 years to collect data on every person living in the country on a specific date. The government needs this data to develop policies, plan and run public services, and allocate funding. It also help illustrate differences between various groups of the population.

In England and Wales, the Census is planned and carried out by the Office for National Statistics (ONS). The last Census was held in March 2011.

The census uses a standard set of ethnic groups to collect and classify ethnicity data. The 2011 census used 18 standardised ethnic categories, grouped under 5 broad categories: White, Mixed/Multiple, Asian/Asian British, Black/African/Caribbean/Black British, and Other ethnic group.

Ethnicity data in the 2011 census: main points

The 2011 Census found that, in England and Wales:

  • the total population was 56.1 million
  • of the 5 broad categories, White is the largest, with 48.2 million people (86%)
  • Asian/Asian British is the second largest group, at 4.2 million (7.5%), followed by Black/African/Caribbean/Black British at 1.9 million (3.3%), Mixed/Multiple ethnic groups at 1.2 million (2.2%), and Other ethnic groups at 564,000 (1%)
  • of the 18 detailed categories, White British is the largest (80.5%), followed by Other White (4.4%) and Indian (2.5%)

White ethnic group

The 2011 census showed that:

  • White British was the largest White group at 45.1 million people (80.5%)
  • Other White background was the second largest, at 2.5 million (4.4%), followed by Irish at 531,000 (0.9%), and Gypsy or Irish Traveller at 58,000 (0.1%)
  • the White percentage of the population in 2011 has decreased compared with 2001 (91.3%) and 1991 (94.1%)
  • the number of White British and White Irish people decreased between 2001 and 2011, while Other White background increased by 1.1 million (1.8%)

The 2011 census introduced the new category of Gypsy or Irish Traveller, so this was the first time figures for that ethnic group were shown.

Other ethnic groups

The 2011 Census showed that:

  • in the detailed categories, Indian was the third largest ethnic group with 1.4 million people (2.5%), followed by Pakistani at 1.1 million (2%)
  • the remaining ethnic groups each accounted for up to 2% of the population
  • the newly introduced Arab category accounted for 240,000 people (0.4%)

Change in ethnicity over time

The broad White ethnic group decreased over the last 20 years, while the other ethnic minority groups continued to rise since the 1991 Census.

In the detailed ethnic categories:

  • between 2001 and 2011, the Other White category had the largest increase, at 1.1 million people (1.8%)
  • the increase in the Other White group includes people born in Poland (an increase of 0.5 million between 2001 and 2011); in 2011, Polish people were the second largest group of residents not born in the UK
  • between 2001 and 2011, the number of people from Pakistani and Indian backgrounds each increased by around 0.4 million (0.5% and 0.6% respectively).
  • the number of people in the other ethnic groups increased by up to 1 per cent for each
